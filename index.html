Teaching Science Students to Code


Solving the Plump Moose problem on the website

Project Lovelace is an online learning platform for people who want to learn to code. The platform is similar to others like Project Euler or Leetcode but geared towards science students. Our key differentiator is providing intriguing problems from all branches of the natural, social, and mathematical sciences.

Our hope is that science students will be more interested in learning if the problems are more interesting than the typical computer science problems found on Leetcode! And so far, we've had over 10,000 code submissions from users all over the world!

This was a project I tackled with my brother, who has studied physics and computational climate modeling. While he created the problems for the platform, I developed the website and backend.

First, I developed the online judge using Python. We wanted to allow users to submit programs in different languages, which required an extensible design. Of course, the judge also had to be secure. For that, I used Linux Containers (LXC). LXC allowed me to easily sandbox user programs, preventing them from accessing the network, interacting with other files, or hogging system resources.

With the online judge ready, I developed the website. I learned to use the Django web framework along with a handy CSS framework called Bulma. Also on the backend is our PostgreSQL database. We use it to store information about the problems, user accounts, as well as user submissions.

Check it out at projectlovelace.net!

Exploring Collaboration Graphs in the World of Music

A collaboration graph of the entire music world; click to view in high resolution One of the largest communities in the music world; click to view in high resolution

It all started with a question. Who is the Paul Erdős of the music world? Erdős is known for being the most prolific collaborator in mathematics. Meaning he has more paper collaborations than any other mathematician, dead or alive.

I hypothesized was that Snoop Dogg would be the answer. He seems to collaborate with everyone! Including a collaboration with Egyptian superstar Tamer Hosny. To answer this question, I needed data. Lots of data.

Luckily, the massive and freely available MusicBrainz database is just what I needed. I started with some data processing inside the PostgreSQL database. Using SQL, I quickly aggregated all collaborations between artists. Then, I exported the data I needed to begin graph analysis.

I used Python's NetworkX library to find the shortest paths between every pair of artists. This helped me answer the question: who's the Paul Erdős of the music world? Shockingly, my hypothesis was correct! It is indeed Snoop Dogg by a wide margin!

For fun, I continued my analysis further with a graph analysis and visualization tool called Gephi. I found that there were community-finding algorithms that could identify communities within the collaboration graph. I was able to produce some beautiful visuals and learn a lot about graphs in the process.

Check out the slide deck I produced for more details and visuals.

Image Recognition with Convolutional Neural Networks


Sample images from the ImageNet dataset

For the final project of my neural networks course, I teamed up with a friend to build a classifier for the famous ImageNet dataset. We investigated and compared the performance of state-of-the-art convolutional neural networks (CNNs) for image recognition.

We decided to use TensorFlow to classify the Tiny ImageNet dataset, a simpler version of the regular ImageNet dataset. We referred to network structures and techniques used by YOLO and AlexNet and derived simplified versions that we could run on a single GTX 970 GPU card.

Our project involved lots of tuning and experimenting and failing and plenty of troubleshooting. Considering our lack of beefy hardware, we were happy to achieve a top-1 accuracy of 30% in the end.

Geo-visualizing City Activity Using Live Tweets


A screenshot of the Migrate app on Android

For my third-year software engineering course, my group and I developed an Android app. The application allows you to spontaneously discover events occurring nearby. By overlaying tweets on Google Maps, we show users where there is lots of activity in their city.

While we did well in the course, this project was an interesting lesson for me. While planning, we assumed that there was enough data for the app to be useful. Near the end of the project, we found out that only a tiny minority of tweets are geotagged. And in that subset of tweets, many of them were businesses advertising their services, not individuals broadcasting impromptu happenings around town. That really taught me the importance of prototyping and challenging assumptions early in the software development life cycle.

On the positive side, we created a goofy video to present the app to the class!
